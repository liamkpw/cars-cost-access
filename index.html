Priced Out: Why Young People in the UK Are Driving Less

Feature story Cost of living Transport inequality

For decades, learning to drive has been treated as a rite of passage in the UK. A symbol of independence, adulthood, and opportunity. But for many young people today, getting behind the wheel is no longer a given. It is becoming a luxury.

Across the UK, the cost of driving has risen sharply over the past decade. Insurance premiums for young drivers have increased significantly, with recent estimates suggesting average annual costs now sit around £1,500–£2,200 per year for new drivers in the UK (Cook,2025) At the same time, used car prices surged during and after the pandemic, rising by approximately 32% year-on-year at their peak in 2021–2022 before stabilising at a higher baseline. (Sharkey,2025) Fuel prices, while fluctuating, have also contributed to the overall cost of driving, with average petrol prices reaching around 134p per litre in 2026, with spikes up to around 179p per litre during recent price surges. (Brumble Guides,2026)

In contrast, wage growth has been far more gradual. Median weekly earnings for full-time employees in the UK were £766.60 in April 2025, according to data from the Office for National Statistics Annual Survey of Hours and Earnings. This figure represents a 5.3% increase compared with 2024, highlighting steady but relatively modest growth in earnings. Over the same period, key driving costs have risen more sharply, creating a growing gap between what people earn and what it costs to drive (Office for National Statistics,2025)

This raises a simple but important question: are young people choosing not to drive, or are they being priced out?

The cost of getting on the road

At first glance, rising driving costs are nothing new. Fuel prices have always fluctuated, and insurance has long been expensive for younger drivers. What has changed is how these costs combine.

Rather than a single expense increasing dramatically, the pressure comes from accumulation. Insurance, fuel, maintenance, and upfront vehicle costs now intersect in a way that makes entry into driving significantly harder than it once was.

One way to understand this shift is through total annual cost. Estimates suggest that running a car in the UK can cost around £3,350 per year on average, covering expenses such as fuel, insurance, tax, and maintenance. (Haines, 2025) With insurance alone accounting for a disproportionately large share for younger drivers, these combined costs place significant financial pressure on new drivers. When these costs are considered together, affordability becomes less about individual price increases and more about overall financial strain.

This cumulative burden is what distinguishes the current moment from previous periods of rising costs. It is not simply that driving is expensive. It is that multiple cost pressures are now felt at once.

Wages vs costs: a growing imbalance

The issue, then, is not just cost, but proportion.

Affordability depends on how costs relate to income. Even where wages are rising, they do not necessarily keep pace with the combined costs of driving. For younger workers, this imbalance is particularly significant.

For example, a newly qualified driver earning around £32,347 per year the median salary for those aged 22–29 equivalent to roughly £2,695 per month before tax, could face insurance costs that take up a significant share of their income. The data also shows that weekly earnings for this age group average around £648 per week, highlighting how much lower incomes are at the start of working life compared to later stages. (Hilton,n.d) This lower income base makes it more difficult to absorb high upfront costs such as insurance. When fuel and maintenance are added, the total cost of driving can take up a substantial share of earnings.

This shifts driving from a manageable expense to a financial risk. Unexpected costs, such as repairs or premium increases, become harder to absorb, particularly for those without savings.

The result is not simply reduced affordability, but increased financial uncertainty around car ownership.

More than a lifestyle choice

Declining rates of driving among young people are often explained through lifestyle changes. Increasing urbanisation, improved public transport, and shifting attitudes towards car ownership are all commonly cited.

While these factors are real, they do not fully explain the scale of the shift.

Licence holding among young people has declined over time, with figures showing a drop from approximately 75% of 21–29-year-olds holding a licence in 1994 to around 63% by 2014, over a period of roughly 20 years. (UK Parliament, 2021) While this trend is sometimes framed as a cultural shift, it coincides with rising costs across multiple aspects of car ownership.

This overlap suggests that economic factors are playing a central role. Opting out of driving may appear to be a choice, but in many cases it reflects limited financial capacity.

Why it matters

The implications of this shift extend beyond individual convenience. In many parts of the UK, particularly outside major cities, access to a car is closely tied to access to opportunity.

In areas with limited public transport, reliance on private vehicles remains high. In the most rural parts of England, around 95% of households in villages, hamlets and isolated dwellings have access to at least one car (Department for Transport,2022), highlighting a strong dependence on private transport for daily life. Without access to a vehicle, commuting options become more restricted, often increasing travel time and reducing job flexibility.

This has a direct impact on employment access. Research shows that 57% of the working-age population in England live in areas with low public transport access to jobs within a 45-minute commute (Benson, M., & Reardon, L. 2025), meaning many people are unable to easily reach employment without a car. These challenges are particularly pronounced in rural and semi-urban areas, where limited transport options can increase travel times and restrict access to a wider range of jobs.

In this sense, car ownership is not simply a matter of convenience. It is closely tied to economic participation.

A quieter form of inequality

Discussions of generational inequality in the UK tend to focus on housing, student debt, and wage stagnation. Transport, by comparison, receives far less attention.

Yet mobility plays a fundamental role in everyday life. The ability to travel for work, education, and social activity shapes access to opportunity in ways that are often overlooked.

What makes this issue particularly notable is how gradually it has developed. There has been no single defining moment, but rather a steady increase in pressure across multiple cost areas. Over time, this has reshaped who can realistically afford to drive.

Conclusion

Young people in the UK are not simply turning away from driving. For many, the option is becoming less accessible.

As costs continue to rise and wages struggle to keep pace, car ownership is increasingly shaped by financial capacity. This challenges the idea that declining driving rates reflect changing preferences alone.

Instead, it points to a broader structural issue. One where economic pressure influences behaviour, and where access to mobility is unevenly distributed.

Driving may once have symbolised independence. Increasingly, it is becoming a marker of who can afford it.

Both stories ask the same underlying question: what happens when mobility becomes unevenly available?

From personal affordability to national infrastructure, access shapes the story.

The EV Divide: Who Actually Has Access to Electric Cars in the UK?

Feature story EV transition Regional inequality

The transition to electric vehicles is often presented as a clear sign of progress. Across the UK, the number of electric cars on the road has increased rapidly, with more than 1.5 million fully electric vehicles in use as of 2025, accounting for around 4.5% of all cars (Lauren, 2025). Public charging infrastructure has expanded alongside this growth, with over 80,000 devices installed nationwide, reflecting a sharp rise in provision in recent years.

At first glance, the trajectory appears straightforward. More electric vehicles, more charging points, and a steady move away from petrol and diesel.

But beneath this narrative of growth lies a more uneven reality.

Growth at a national level

Government policy has played a central role in shaping this transition. Plans to phase out the sale of new petrol and diesel cars by 2030 (UK Government, n.d.) have accelerated both investment and adoption. EV registrations have increased significantly, with annual battery electric sales rising by approximately 23.9% year on year in 2025. Charging infrastructure has expanded in parallel, growing by around 37% between December 2023 and December 2024.(Tromans, P.2025)

Taken together, these figures suggest a system moving in the right direction. But they also mask an important limitation.

National totals show scale. They do not show access.

Looking beyond the totals

The way infrastructure is experienced in everyday life is far less uniform than headline figures suggest. National growth can create the impression of steady progress, but access to charging is often determined by far more local factors. The availability of nearby charge points, the ease of using them, and the reliability of the network all shape whether electric vehicles feel like a realistic option. In practice, this means that the transition is not experienced evenly. For some, it is already part of daily life. For others, it remains uncertain, inconvenient, or simply out of reach.

The urban advantage

The concentration of infrastructure in urban areas reflects how and where investment is prioritised.

Cities, by their nature, provide the conditions that make charging infrastructure more viable. Higher population density increases usage rates, while shorter distances reduce installation costs. As a result, infrastructure tends to cluster where demand is already strong.

Urban areas now account for approximately 82.9% of public EV chargers in England and Wales, according to government data. (Department for Transport,2026) This concentration reinforces itself. More chargers make electric vehicles more practical, which in turn increases demand for further infrastructure.

Over time, this creates a reinforcing cycle of access and expansion.

Rural constraints

Outside of urban centres, the situation is markedly different.

Lower population density, longer travel distances, and higher installation costs slow the rollout of infrastructure. As a result, rural areas often see fewer charging points relative to their population.

Only around 17.1% of public chargers are located in rural areas, meaning the majority of infrastructure is concentrated in more populated parts of the country. (Department for Transport, 2026)

For many households, home charging offers a potential solution. But this is not universally available. Around 32.8% of UK households do not have access to off-street parking, meaning they must rely on public infrastructure. (Zapmap, 2026)

Where that infrastructure is limited, the transition to electric vehicles becomes significantly more difficult.

A question of access

The result is a growing divide in access to electric mobility.

In some areas, charging infrastructure is visible, accessible, and integrated into everyday life. In others, it remains sparse and difficult to rely on. This uneven distribution shapes who can realistically participate in the transition.

Access, in this sense, becomes just as important as availability.

What the data shows

A direct comparison between regions highlights the scale of this imbalance.

The best-served area, London, has 338.1 chargers per 100,000 people, while the least-served region, Northern Ireland, has just 57.5 per 100,000. (Department for Transport, 2026)

This difference is not marginal. It represents a structural gap in access to infrastructure.

Without adjusting for population, this disparity is largely hidden. But once scaled, it becomes central to understanding how the transition is unfolding.

Why it matters

Transport is closely tied to access to employment, services, and social participation. If access to charging infrastructure is uneven, so too is access to electric mobility.

As the UK moves towards phasing out petrol and diesel vehicles, this imbalance becomes more significant. Those in well-served areas are able to transition more easily, while others face practical limitations.

This raises broader questions about how policy is implemented. A transition designed to be national in scope may, in practice, operate very differently at a local level.

Conclusion

The growth of electric vehicles in the UK tells a story of progress. But it is not a complete one.

Looking more closely at how infrastructure is distributed reveals a more complex reality. Access varies significantly depending on location, shaping who can benefit from the transition and who cannot.

As the UK continues to move towards an electric future, the question is no longer just how much infrastructure exists.

It is where it exists — and who it serves.
